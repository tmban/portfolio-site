Context

The workflow was held together by spreadsheets and hope

Verde is a B2B ESG reporting platform built for Meridian Capital, a real estate investment manager managing sustainability disclosures across GRESB, EPRA, INREV and SFDR. Reporting sat at the centre of how portfolio data became client-facing output — but the entire workflow was manual, fragmented and ungoverned.

This project redesigned it end-to-end: how reports are created, reviewed, approved and delivered — bringing consistency, governance and trust to a process that previously relied on spreadsheets and individual discipline.

The stakes made it worse

Before redesigning anything, I mapped how ESG metrics were defined and grouped across frameworks — Environmental, Social and Governance each behaved differently in GRESB vs INREV vs EPRA. That foundation shaped every structural decision downstream.

ESG metric taxonomy across Environmental, Social and Governance dimensions. Definitions varied significantly by framework — getting this right before touching the interface was non-negotiable.

Despite portfolio data living in the platform, consultants constantly left it to do reporting work. Reports were downloaded, reformatted in Excel, manually cross-checked and reassembled before being shared. No central record. No version control. No confidence in what was current.

"Not being able to trust a platform is really frustrating… you’ve got to use a lot of manual stuff on Excel… it doubles the time and it’s quite stressful."

Reporting Consultant — Discovery Round 1

The stakes made it worse

These reports reached lawyers, senior stakeholders and regulatory reviewers. Users couldn't trust the platform without manually verifying outputs first — and when something looked off, there was no audit trail to explain why. The problem wasn't just friction. It was a trust deficit at the heart of a high-stakes workflow.

01
No single source of truth
The same KPI showing different numbers across GRESB, INREV and annual reports — assembled separately, reconciled manually, trusted by nobody.
02
Constant manual overhead
Hours of xlookup reconciliation per cycle for funds with 120–200 assets. Every report cycle, from scratch.
03
No governance structure
Reports could reach clients without any senior verification. No sign-off process — just individual discipline keeping things from going wrong.
04
No status visibility
Status lived in inboxes and sticky notes. Knowing what was in progress, under review or ready meant chasing someone directly.
05
Zero institutional handover
When a consultant changed accounts, all client-specific context went with them. The next person started cold every time.
06
Growing client scrutiny
Clients — and their lawyers — were asking harder questions. Consultants had no way to trace a number back to source.
How Might We

How might we give every consultant one place to manage reports — with clear status, structured approval and consistent data across every framework — without adding process overhead?

Research

More people were affected than we initially thought

Before wireframing anything, I needed to understand the full reporting lifecycle, expose hidden dependencies and establish what had to change first. The reporting process touched several distinct stakeholders — internally and externally. Treating it as a single-user feature would've been the wrong call. Mapping the full ecosystem early meant every subsequent design decision was grounded in the actual operational reality.

Six users, six completely different definitions of success

Six user types drove every design decision. Each had a completely different relationship with reporting — different ownership, different urgency, different definition of a good outcome.

Reporting Consultant
Day-to-day owner of report delivery. Highest operational load — most exposed to manual friction and cross-framework inconsistency. Time is the resource they have least of.
Reporting Team
Specialist support brought in to produce bespoke outputs. Bridges the gap between raw platform data and polished client-facing reports — but only works well when the brief, data and timeline are clear from the start.
Data Team
Receives the final output. Increasingly aware of reporting standards and increasingly likely to ask hard questions — and expect traceable answers.
Client ESG Director
Receives the final output. Increasingly aware of reporting standards and increasingly likely to ask hard questions — and expect traceable answers.
Fund Manager
Performance-focused owner of a fund's ESG obligations. Needs clear, asset-level data to make renovation and allocation decisions — and accountability when things don't add up.
Property Manager
Closest to the asset. Responsible for keeping building-level data accurate and acted upon. Success means their properties are investment-ready — not just compliant on paper.

Mapping the process revealed how much the product was missing

Breaking down the full lifecycle revealed stages the product wasn't supporting at all — requirement gathering, framework interpretation, internal QA, handover. Mapping it exposed where ownership broke down, where rework loops appeared and what was generating the most wasted effort.

Knowing who's in the room changes what you build

I mapped reporting requests from initial onboarding all the way to client delivery. What came back was a picture of constant context-switching — between requirements, data gathering, validation and output prep — with the platform only supporting a fraction of it.

Key Finding

The platform had real advantages in data quality visibility and comparative analytics that competitors hadn't cracked. The play was to close the automation and traceability gaps while doubling down on those strengths.

Testing our assumptions before committing to a direction

Before wireframing, I documented the core design assumptions driving our thinking — then structured a research plan to validate or challenge each one. Four hypotheses shaped the project most.

Hypothesis 1
Consultants need one governed place to manage all reports
We believe that
Consultants are managing reports across multiple tools and have no structured way to track status, ownership or approval — and this is causing delivery errors and wasted time.
To verify
Review existing workflows, interview consultants on how they currently track report status, observe handoff behaviour between junior and senior consultants.
We are right if
Majority of consultants describe manually tracking report status outside the platform and cite ownership gaps as a source of errors or delays.
Hypothesis 2
The same number needs to appear consistently across every framework
We believe that
Cross-framework inconsistency — the same KPI showing different values in GRESB, INREV and annual reports — is the single biggest driver of client distrust and consultant rework.
To verify
Analyse past reports for value discrepancies across frameworks. Interview consultants about reconciliation effort and probe which errors have reached clients.
We are right if
Research surfaces multiple examples of cross-framework number mismatches and consultants confirm this is a recurring, high-stress part of delivery.

Not all pain is equal — separating what breaks delivery from what's just annoying

Synthesising the journey maps, swim lanes and process map gave us a clear severity hierarchy — separating friction that was annoying from friction that was actively breaking delivery quality, team trust and client relationships.

Critical
Data inconsistency across frameworks
The most cited issue. The same figure showing differently across GRESB, INREV and annual reports — directly damaging client trust.
Critical
No audit trail or traceability
With lawyers reviewing outputs, consultants needed to explain every figure. The platform had no way to trace a number to its source.
Significant
No approval governance
Reports reached clients without a formal sign-off stage. The workflow relied entirely on individual discipline to prevent errors.
Significant
Invisible report status
No shared view of progress. Knowing what was in-progress, under review or ready meant interrupting a colleague to ask.
Tolerable
Difficult to find historical reports
No pinning, no favouriting, limited filtering — slow to navigate for consultants managing 20+ active reports across multiple clients.
Tolerable
External tools for analytics
Power BI dashboards had to be opened separately to validate trends. Manageable today, but growing more disruptive as report volumes increased.

Testing

Two rounds of testing — and a few humbling surprises

Prototype sessions tested whether users could complete reporting tasks without touching a spreadsheet. The early signal was strong — the structure matched how consultants already thought about their work. But the details told a different story.

R1
Round 1 — Concept validation
Low-fidelity flows, focused on navigation, status labels and upload. Biggest finding: "pending review" was read as a data quality flag, not an approval state. A small label — a significant misread in a governance-critical workflow.
R2
Round 2 — High-fidelity prototype
Full flow including pinned reports, filters, report detail and Power BI embed. Surfaced the need for bulk download and clearer framework tag hierarchy. Both fixed before engineering handoff.
↺
Key iterations
Report cards redesigned to lead with status and team. Upload split into two steps after single-screen forms caused abandonment. Pinned reports added after consultants described returning to the same 3–5 reports every single day.

Speed without visibility isn't an improvement — testing taught us that

Users were just as concerned about preserving governance control as they were about removing friction. Faster wasn't better if it meant bypassing review. That shaped every decision around status, permissions and report handling.

"When I can see the report status and who last touched it, I can have a much better conversation with my client about timelines. Right now I'm just guessing."

Design

Reporting as a live workspace, not a document store

The shipped product combined report generation, approval governance and live analytics in one connected experience. Reports weren't files at the end of a process anymore — they were active, status-tracked objects at the centre of day-to-day delivery.

01
Reports became easier to manage, review and approve
Report cards surface framework, ownership, status and key ESG metrics before you even open a report. No more clicking in just to check whether something needs attention. Approval states are visible at card level — making handoffs between consultants and senior reviewers explicit and traceable.
Under Review Approved Rejected
→Approval state visible before opening — status badges at card level so consultants can triage without entering each report
→Compare progress across frameworks — tab-based filtering (All, Favourites, Under Review, Approved, Rejected) isolates what needs action instantly
→Team visibility at a glance — assigned consultant avatars and last-activity date on every card, no need to open
02
Personalised dashboard — the reports relevant to you, first
The home dashboard surfaces reports relevant to each consultant first — with chart previews, framework logos and key metric snapshots on the card itself. Favourite reports for quick access. See what needs a response before opening anything.
→Real chart previews on cards — actual data visualisations give a quick read before opening, replacing the need to enter a report just to check the numbers
→Scope 1/2/3 emissions inline — key sustainability KPIs on the card itself for faster orientation across multiple reports
03
Report generation became part of the product
Report generation now starts inside the platform — not in a template on someone's desktop. Users select a framework, configure parameters through guided steps and generate structured outputs directly from portfolio data. Breaking it into smaller decisions reduced setup errors and made complex frameworks navigable.
→Framework-led generation — select GRI, GRESB, EPRA or CSRD upfront so the rest of the flow is scoped correctly from the start
→Guided parameter setup — GHG emissions, energy, water and waste as icon-led options, not a form to fill in
04
Focused multi-step parameter configuration
Step two is a single decision — clean icon-led options, no configuration complexity. A progress bar communicates position. Back navigation is always there. In testing, multi-field forms caused abandonment; this didn't.
→One decision per step — prevents the decision paralysis that caused users to abandon complex multi-field forms in testing
→Progress bar keeps users oriented — always visible so users know how many steps remain
05
Generated reports became interactive, not static
Generated reports open inside a dedicated view with headline KPIs, an AI narrative summary and chart breakdowns — everything needed to understand performance without leaving the platform or opening a separate document. Approve, share or export from the same screen.
→KPI summary inside report view — Total Energy, Carbon Footprint and Water Usage with YoY comparison built in
→Narrative insights beside charts — AI-generated summary explains trends so consultants can read and act without further analysis
→Net Zero trajectory visible — On Track / Net Zero 2050 status shown inline so stakeholders confirm alignment without a separate document
06
External tools became part of the reporting workflow
Consultants regularly switched to Power BI or Tableau to validate trends mid-report. Now those dashboards embed directly inside the report page — alongside Verde's metadata, approvals and team context. The analytics stack stays intact; the context switching goes away.
→Embedded dashboards without leaving Verde — Power BI reports displayed inline alongside Verde's metadata, approvals and team context
→Connect to existing enterprise tools — integrations extend the reporting workflow into the tools teams already use

Reflection

Designing reporting as a system, not a page

Reporting touched multiple teams, multiple trust decisions and several hidden operational dependencies that only surfaced once the full workflow was mapped. The solution worked because it reduced fragmentation while respecting how enterprise reporting actually operates — messy handoffs, legal scrutiny and all.

01
Governance design is UX design
The approval workflow was the highest-impact change in the product — not a back-end concern. Getting labels, permission states and role boundaries right required as much rigour as any visual surface. The three-status model was earned through iteration, not obvious from the start.
02
Consistency is a trust mechanism
Knowing the same number would appear across every framework output was the single biggest driver of user confidence. Data consistency had more impact on trust than anything visual.
03
Status visibility reduces anxiety at scale
Seeing every report's status from one screen sounds mundane. For teams running 20–30 reports under deadline pressure, it was psychologically significant — and one of the most appreciated things we shipped.
04
Design the handoff, not just the interface
The pain wasn't in any single screen — it was in the transitions between people and teams. Making those transitions structured and visible solved a class of problems that no individual screen could address alone.
