Arbor

Redesigning how a complex asset platform represents, connects and manages the real world.

Context

The foundation was broken

Arbor is a portfolio management platform used by Lumina Capital to track, report on and manage real assets — from investment funds down to individual meters within buildings. When I joined the project, the platform was losing client trust. Reports were wrong, setup was unpredictable, and users couldn't make sense of the data in front of them.

The problems ran deeper than a difficult onboarding experience. The underlying data model forced real-world assets — solar farms, airports, mixed-use buildings — into a structure built for commercial real estate. Users couldn't see how their assets connected to one another, couldn't trace where values came from, and couldn't understand why data looked the way it did. On top of that, setup happened almost entirely offline in spreadsheets, and the platform had no way to represent who should be doing what.

Scope

End-to-end redesign spanning data model architecture, onboarding flows, asset hierarchy visualisation, lifecycle status tracking and user tier structures — from foundational IA definition through to validated, shipped UI.

Six interconnected problems, one broken foundation

Research across seven role types — Fund Managers, Property Managers, Building Managers, ESG Directors, Data Team members, Customer Success Managers and Consultants — surfaced six distinct failure modes. Each was distinct on the surface. At the root, they shared the same cause: the platform had been built on structural assumptions that didn't reflect how real assets worked.

01
All-or-nothing setup
Onboarding only happened when every detail was known. Partial data couldn't be saved in-platform, so everything lived in spreadsheets until it was complete. This was a design gap, not a client behaviour problem.
02
No transparency for clients
Clients couldn't see what was missing, what progress was being made, or what remained before they could use the product. This directly damaged time-to-value and was a leading factor in early disengagement.
03
No way to understand connections
Users could see data in tables but couldn't understand how entities related, how values were inherited, or why a number looked the way it did. Tracing anything back to source required manual investigation.
04
Conflicting data sources
Multiple versions of setup spreadsheets circulated between teams. By the time data was uploaded, it was frequently out of date or contradictory — and there was no way to resolve which version was current.
05
Infrastructure assets forced into wrong shapes
The model assumed commercial real estate. Solar farms, airports and distributed energy networks had no clean fit — leading to structurally incorrect data from day one, silently corrupting downstream reporting.
06
Errors compounded over time
Setup mistakes weren't caught until reporting inconsistencies surfaced — often months later. By then, unwinding the errors was disproportionately expensive.

"We can't just let that happen silently — if building-level data conflicts with what's set at property level, users need to know."

Research

The errors weren't in the data — they were in how the system represented the world

Research ran across all seven role types. The goal was to understand how each role experienced the platform — and crucially, how real assets were managed, structured and reported on in practice, separately from how Arbor modelled them. The gap between those two things was where every problem lived.

All sessions were stored in Dovetail — its AI features helped surface themes and tag failure points across seven distinct role types simultaneously, which would have taken significantly longer to cross-reference manually. I still read back through the source material for each role, because the differences in how a Fund Manager and a Building Manager described the same broken workflow were exactly the kind of signal that clustering tends to lose.

Real-life scenarios — from storyboards to decision flows

To move beyond individual pain points, I built out end-to-end scenarios showing how work actually moved across roles. Storyboards captured the social layer. Decision flows made the within-platform and outside-platform moments explicit, surfacing where the current system forced decisions offline that should have been supported in-product.

Claude was used to draft the initial scenario scaffolding — mapping role sequences and handoff points from research notes before committing to a visual format. That let me pressure-test the logic of each flow quickly, and catch gaps in the narrative before they became gaps in the design. Relume.io helped turn those flows into structured sitemaps and wireframe scaffolds, giving engineering and product an early reference point before high-fidelity work began.

How Might We

Rebuild Arbor's foundations so that assets are represented correctly, users can understand how their data connects, onboarding is owned by the client rather than managed offline, and different user types can do what they need to do — without stepping on each other?

Ideation

A platform rebuilt from its foundations

Before building screens, I needed to agree on what the system should stand for. Four principles kept every decision grounded across the engagement.

In-platform over offline
Every step that currently happened in Excel or email should happen in Arbor. The system should own the process, not just receive its output.
Structure should be visible, not inferred
Users shouldn't have to deduce how their assets connect from tables of numbers. The platform should surface relationships, inheritance and data flow directly.
Errors should be caught at entry, not discovered at reporting
Structural problems compound over time. Validation belongs at setup — not as a cleanup task after months of incorrect data has accumulated.
The model should reflect how real assets behave
Infrastructure assets, mixed-use buildings and multi-fund properties all exist. The hierarchy should accommodate reality — not force reality into commercial real estate assumptions.

Six phases, each building on the last

The work was scoped across six design phases, starting with foundational IA alignment and progressively layering in user flows, data definitions, the visual canvas, lifecycle statuses, user tier structures and bulk upload. Solution

What we built

The redesign addressed four interconnected problems that were collectively undermining trust in Arbor. Each solution was designed to work independently — and to reinforce the others.

01
Making connections visible — the Arbor canvas
One of the most consistent findings across research was that people couldn't understand how their assets related to one another. They could see data in tables, but they couldn't trace where values came from or understand how data flowed through the hierarchy. The solution was a visual canvas built on ReactFlow — a spatial, interactive view of the entity hierarchy. Rather than inferring connections from tables, users could see the full structure directly and follow data from report back to source.
→Entity relationships visualised spatially — users see how Investment Entities link to Properties, Buildings, Spaces and Meters at a glance
→Data flow made explicit — users can trace how values propagate from source through to report without manual investigation
→Inheritance vs manual entry distinguished — users can see whether a value was set at this level or passed down from a parent

02
Onboarding moved from back-office admin task to customer-led setup
The original onboarding process was managed entirely offline: Customer Success Managers orchestrated every step using a legacy platform, spreadsheets and direct database operations. Clients had no visibility. Setup only happened when every detail was confirmed, then uploaded in a single fragile batch. When something was wrong, everything downstream was wrong. The redesign shifted ownership to the customer, with the platform handling structure creation, user assignment and permissions automatically.
→Simple and Detailed asset states — Simple assets allow clients to establish hierarchy before all data is confirmed; Detailed assets are fully validated and feed directly into reporting
→Bulk uploads at every entity level — with platform-side validation on import rather than silent acceptance of a broken dataset
→Progress visible at every level — clients and CSMs can both see what's configured, what's missing and what's blocking activation
→CSMs moved from execution to oversight — removing the fragile offline coordination layer that caused cascading errors

03
The information architecture — new entity levels, each with clear inheritance rules
Before a single screen was designed, I worked with data architects to map every entity type the platform needed to represent: Investment Entity, Property, Building, Internal/External Space and Meter. Each level required explicit attribute sets and clear rules governing what it could inherit or override from its parent. This IA document became the cross-functional contract — the point at which design, engineering and data all agreed on what the system represented before anyone built anything.
→5 entity levels mapped — Investment Entity → Property → Building → Space → Meter, each with explicit attributes
→Financials and reporting separated — ownership %, GAV and sector reporting at Property level; consumption at Meter level
→Lifecycle status tracked at every level — current and previous statuses surfaced per entity, enabling accurate renovation and construction tracking

New information architecture displayed through the platform UI.

04
Lifecycle statuses and user tiers — supporting how assets and people actually behave
Assets don't stay static: buildings go under renovation, spaces get repurposed, meters are decommissioned. But the original model had no way to represent this. I designed a lifecycle status system tracked at every entity level, with current and previous statuses both surfaced — giving the platform the temporal context it had always been missing. Alongside this, a structured user tier system mapped platform permissions to real-world responsibilities across all seven role types, from Fund Managers to external Consultants.
→Lifecycle tracked at every hierarchy level — renovation, construction and decommission periods reflected correctly rather than skewing active portfolio data
→Approval workflows gated by tier — financial sign-off and entity activation require the appropriate access level
→External users handled cleanly — Consultants and third parties receive scoped access without exposing the full data model

Reflection

What this project taught me about designing data platforms

The hardest UX problems in data platforms are the ones that happen before anyone opens a dashboard. Setup screens and onboarding flows rarely get the investment that analytics and reporting UI receives — but the quality of every downstream interaction depends entirely on how well the foundation was laid. This project made that argument repeatedly, and winning it changed the scope of what design was responsible for.

01
Workarounds reveal missing features, not broken users
The Excel spreadsheet wasn't a bad habit — it was a rational response to a platform that couldn't support partial information. Every workaround in user research pointed to a genuine gap in the product. Treating them as signals rather than noise changed what I built.
02
Visibility is a structural requirement
Making things visible — asset connections, setup progress, data inheritance, user permissions — wasn't a UX polish layer. It was foundational to the platform working at all. Users don't trust data they can't trace, and they don't complete processes they can't see the end of.
03
Multi-user journeys require multi-user design
Arbor touched seven different role types, none of whom had full visibility of the others' steps. Designing for a single user would have missed most of the actual friction. The solution required designing for handoffs, permissions and shared state — not just individual screens.
04
The IA document was a design deliverable, not a prerequisite
Mapping the full information architecture before touching UI surfaced contradictions in the data model that would have derailed UI work later. The IA became a cross-functional contract — the point at which design, engineering and data all agreed on what the system represented before anyone built anything.
