Introduction

Data visibility that drives decisions

Plio is a utility data management platform built for Crestfield Group, a property and infrastructure services firm. Invoice processing sat at the very start of the data pipeline — before any analytics or ESG reporting could happen. It was also almost entirely manual, fragile, and impossible to scale.

This project covered discovery, requirements definition and early solution shaping for an AI-assisted extraction experience: designing the workflow, validation logic and fallback patterns before a single line of code was written.

"Automated invoice parsing would cut the process in half."

Collection Team — Discovery Research
Problem

Manual processing was the norm — and everyone had adapted their work around it

Before any analytics or reporting could happen, invoice data had to be collected, interpreted, attributed and validated by hand. Collection teams spent significant time transcribing values, translating non-English invoices, interpreting ambiguous dates and cross-referencing historical data. Clients were expected to pre-process invoices before submission — coordinating between property managers, asset managers and suppliers before anything reached the platform.

01
Manual transcription at scale
Every invoice value was manually keyed. Across hundreds of invoices per client, this compounded error risk and drained collection team capacity with no mechanism to absorb growth.
02
Non-English invoices required translation
International portfolios generated invoices in multiple languages. Teams translated manually before data entry could begin — adding time, ambiguity and inconsistency across the dataset.
03
Date fields were routinely ambiguous
Billing dates and consumption periods were frequently conflated. Without a standard interpretation, teams made judgment calls — introducing inconsistency that was invisible until it surfaced in reporting.
04
Multi-meter attribution done by hand
Around 15–20% of invoices cover multiple meters — each requiring manual attribution. Split allocations had no system support and were handled in spreadsheets, cross-checked against historical patterns by hand.
05
Client pre-processing required before submission
Clients were expected to organise, label and partially process invoices before sending them. This delayed data availability and put quality burden on external teams who had no platform access.
06
Process scaled linearly with portfolio size
Every new client and expanding portfolio directly increased operational cost. There was no mechanism to absorb growth — the problem compounded as the business grew.
How Might We

How might we automate the ingestion of utility invoices while preserving accuracy, traceability and user confidence — without forcing rigid workflows that break in real-world scenarios?

Discovery

Research wasn't about how teams wished invoices worked — it was about how they actually do

I spoke with collection and data entry teams, property managers, asset managers, consultants and internal platform and product teams. The goal was to map where time was lost, where automation would break down, and where human judgment was irreplaceable and needed to stay in the loop.

01
Invoice diversity is the norm, not the edge case
Invoices vary widely in format, language, structure and attribution. Multi-meter invoices, credit notes and ambiguous date fields appear constantly — any system that can't handle them is a system that fails regularly. This was later confirmed in engineering testing across invoices from five countries and multiple languages, all of which had to be handled without provider-specific prompting.
02
80% automation is valuable — but only with strong 20% fallback
Users were clear: partial automation is welcome, but only if what the AI can't do is handled just as well. A system that fails silently on 20% of invoices is worse than no automation at all.
03
Meter attribution is harder than extraction
Parsing numbers is relatively tractable. Correctly assigning those numbers to the right meter — especially with split allocations — is where most errors actually occur. Attribution needed to be a first-class problem, not a footnote.
04
Immediate feedback is a trust requirement, not a nice-to-have
Users expected to upload an invoice and immediately see parsed results. Any delay between upload and visibility increased manual double-checking and eroded trust in the system's output — before they'd even seen it.
05
Excel is the benchmark — and the fallback
If the system felt restrictive or opaque, users reverted to spreadsheets. This was stated directly in research. Any solution had to feel at least as flexible and transparent as Excel — otherwise adoption wouldn't happen.

Competitive context — table stakes vs real opportunity

Competitor research showed that multi-format parsing, immediate parsed data visibility and automatic error detection were already expected capabilities — not differentiators.

Competitive Opportunity

Competitors had solved basic extraction. What they hadn't solved was ESG-native attribution — connecting invoice data directly to asset hierarchies, reporting frameworks and portfolio-level analytics. That integration was the real differentiator, not the parsing itself.

Ideation

Automation only works if users can see inside it

Four principles governed every design decision — keeping the system honest about what it could and couldn't do, and ensuring the human override path was always as good as the automated one.

01
Automate by default, support exceptions explicitly
The common case should be effortless. But credit notes, ambiguous dates and multi-meter splits must be handled with equal care — not treated as errors.
02
Keep the invoice visible during validation
Users need to verify extracted values against the source. Any design that hides the original invoice forces a parallel manual step — negating the automation benefit.
03
Make confidence and uncertainty explicit
A confident-looking wrong answer is worse than an honest uncertain one. The system should always communicate what it knows, what it's guessing, and what it couldn't determine.
04
Design for real-world complexity, not ideal data
Non-English invoices, scanned PDFs, missing fields and split allocations are standard. The system needs to handle reality, not a sanitised version of it.

Phased delivery — parity first, intelligence later

Rather than over-promising full AI automation from day one, the project defined a phased approach grounded in what was buildable and what users actually needed at each stage. Phase 1 prioritised competitive parity and reliable 80% automation. Later phases added intelligence on top of a stable foundation.

Phase 1 — Must Have
Competitive parity & 80% automation
  • Multi-format invoice upload
  • AI parsing with immediate data display
  • Field-level confidence indicators
  • Split-screen review layout
  • Inline manual override for any field
  • Meter attribution with manual reassignment
  • Credit invoice and date handling
Phase 2 — Enhancements
UX improvements & integrations
  • Batch upload with per-file status tracking
  • Historical pattern matching for attribution
  • Non-English invoice translation layer (validated in engineering — UK, DE, US, IT, ES tested)
  • Audit trail for override decisions
  • Client workflow integrations
  • Percentage-based split allocation controls
Phase 3+ — Intelligence
Portfolio intelligence & ESG integration
  • Portfolio-wide anomaly detection
  • Direct ESG data pipeline to analytics
  • Hybrid AI + consultant validation workflows
  • Predictive attribution from consumption patterns
  • Automated cross-referencing against historical baselines

Testing assumptions before defining requirements

Hypothesis 1
Users will trust AI output if uncertainty is surfaced honestly
We believe that
The barrier to AI adoption isn't the technology — it's opacity. Users who can't see why the system reached a conclusion will override it by default, regardless of accuracy.
To verify
Show users mock parsed results with and without confidence indicators. Measure override rate and whether explanation changes user behaviour.
We are right if
Override rate decreases when the system explains its certainty level — and users express greater confidence in results they can interrogate.
Hypothesis 2
The invoice must stay visible during the entire review workflow
We believe that
Users will not trust parsed values they can't cross-reference against the source. Any layout that hides the original invoice will create a parallel manual checking step — defeating the automation benefit.
To verify
Test two layouts: extracted data only vs split-screen with source document visible. Observe whether users without inline access open the invoice in a separate tab.
We are right if
Users without source document access consistently open it externally — adding steps the design was supposed to eliminate.

Both assumptions held in early concept testing. Here's what changed in the design approach:

Learning 1
Confidence surfacing is a prerequisite for adoption, not a phase 2 feature
We observed
When confidence levels were visible, users focused their review on flagged fields and accepted high-confidence results readily. Without them, they reviewed everything — the same effort as manual entry.
Therefore, we will
Confidence indicators ship in v1 — the system's usefulness depends on them. Not a later enhancement.
Learning 2
Split-screen is the default review state — not a power user option
We observed
Every user who lacked inline source access opened the invoice in a separate window within 60 seconds of review. The source document is a crutch users cannot give up — so the design should support it, not fight it.
Therefore, we will
Invoice visible left, extracted data right — always. Not a preference setting, the default state for all review.
Engineering

The engineering team picked it up — and the design constraints held

After the design phase concluded, the engineering team ran a structured ~3-week investigation into multi-meter invoice support — one of the core edge cases the roadmap had flagged. Around 15–20% of invoices contain multiple meters, and getting the right value to the right meter is meaningfully harder than single-meter extraction. A hackathon compared three approaches across invoices from five countries and multiple languages.

All three approaches converged on roughly the same parsing success rate. The team chose the OCR + LLM path — staying on Azure, offering better model consistency and temperature control. Crucially, the investigation confirmed that pre- and post-processing steps were as important as model choice: normalising dates and consumption values, handling locale-specific number formatting, whitelisting valid meter types, and filtering out invoices that shouldn't be automated at all (very large files, low-quality scans). The design principle of knowing what the system can and cannot support — and routing each cleanly — mapped directly onto what the engineers needed to build.

A generic prompt approach, which had previously failed, worked with the newer model generation. The working hypothesis is that better pre-processing reduces the context burden enough that the model can generalise rather than needing provider-specific prompting. The pipeline is currently being implemented.

The decision to build a pre-processing filter — routing genuinely unsupported invoices to manual handling rather than forcing them through automation — came directly from the design phase. When the engineers ran testing, that same boundary turned out to be where the model performance degraded. The design had already drawn the line in the right place.

The Solution

Automation with guardrails — not a black box, not a form

The designed system introduces an AI-assisted ingestion pipeline that makes what the AI did — and didn't do — explicit at every step. Rather than hiding uncertainty behind a confident-looking result, the interface surfaces confidence levels, flags uncertain fields for human review, and keeps the original invoice visible throughout. The 80% the AI handles reliably is seamless. The 20% it can't is handled with equal care.

Uploading as simple as logging in and dropping a file
The entry point needs to feel effortless. Users should never have to pre-process an invoice before submitting it — the system handles format diversity, batch uploads and immediate confirmation. Friction here poisons trust in everything downstream.
→Multi-format support — PDFs, scans, images and spreadsheets accepted without pre-processing
→Batch upload capability — multiple invoices processed in parallel with per-file status tracking
→Immediate confirmation — upload success shown instantly, not after parsing completes
→No context switching — upload lives in the same workflow context as the asset it belongs to

→Known meter matching — extracted values automatically matched to registered meters where identifiers align

→Unmatched values surfaced, not dropped — values without a confirmed meter flagged for review, never silently lost

Outcome

A validated direction — grounded in real operational complexity

This project was a design and discovery engagement. The deliverable wasn't a shipped product — it was the foundation that made building the right product possible. Five stakeholder groups aligned on requirements that had previously been assumed. The 80% automation target was validated rather than guessed at. A phased roadmap gave engineering a clear, sequenced path. And every design decision traced back to research or concept testing — not opinion.

The Solution section shows the UI design handed off at the end of this phase. Engineering picked it up from there — and as the Engineering section shows, the constraints the design established held up when it came to building.

Reflection

Automation fails when it hides uncertainty

The temptation in AI-assisted design is to make the technology look more capable than it is. Every confident-looking result that turns out to be wrong teaches the user to distrust the system entirely. The right approach is the opposite — surface uncertainty honestly, make the override path seamless, and let trust build through accuracy rather than appearances.

01
The 20% fallback is as important as the 80% automation
Most AI feature design focuses on the success path. This project spent equal effort on what happens when parsing fails — because a seamless fallback is what separates a useful tool from a frustrating one. If the manual path is worse than before, the automation hasn't helped.
02
Diversity in the data means diversity in the design
Invoice formats, languages, structures and attribution patterns vary enormously. Designing for one clean invoice type produces a system that works in demos and fails in production. The research made sure we designed for the messy reality.
03
Discovery that prevents wrong builds is itself a delivery
This project didn't ship UI — it shaped what would be built next. Establishing shared understanding of operational complexity, validating the 80% target and defining a phased roadmap changed the engineering approach before a line of code was written. That's a result.
